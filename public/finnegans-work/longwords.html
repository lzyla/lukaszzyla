The longest words: a multilingual decomposition

Are Joyce's hundred-letter compounds chaos? No — they are mosaics of words from a dozen languages. Automatic decompositions below.

1. Method

I took the 40 longest words of the book (the ten thunderwords among them) and, for each, found every exact substring that is a word in one of 14 dictionaries (English + 13 languages; fragments ≥4 letters, ≥5 for English). From the matches I select a non-overlapping cover of maximal total length. This detects only undistorted fragments — Joyce routinely deforms his borrowings (It. tuono → “tuonn”), so the real multilingual share is higher than measured.

2. Patterns

Mean dictionary coverage of the longest words: 79.6% — the bulk of these monsters is recognisable word-stuff, not random letters. As many as 39 of 40 words contain fragments from at least two languages (including at least one foreign) — the long compounds are systematically multilingual.
